Where are the measure units located?

The size of the circle defines the average number of daily counted counted bikes. The bigger the circle, the more bikes pass every day. Not surprising that streets close to the centre count more byciclists than those who are further away from the center.

When was the data collected?

Not all measure units were active throughout all the years. At Dr.Luises Bro, for example, only the years between 2009 and 2013 were captured. Other streets were only measured during certain months or days of the year. Overall, there is enough data to predict the traffic volume The size of the circles depicts the daily average amount bicycle traffic the year.

How reliable is the data?

The data shows gaps throughout all scales. Not captured hours due to the time shift. (Every year, end of March and end of October, all measure units had no counting between 2am and 3am), or days and weeks due to barriers or construction sites are prevalent for every street and measure unit. Still, the errors in the data itself as missing values or zero-values are small and therefore easy to fix. Still, unequal distributed counted days makes it especially hard to analysis longer time intervalls as years and decades.

The plot below decpicts a typical year: Some streets were not counted at all(grey cells in Englandsvej) others only at certain days during the month (grey cells at Dr. Louises Bro) others counted zero traffic for several days (light yellow cells at Fredensbro between Jan and June) .

Daily Passing Motor Vehicles on Average

Where are the measure units located?

The size of the circle defines the average number of daily counted motor vehicles. The bigger the circle, the more motor vehicles pass every day. Not surprising that the streets with the highest traffic volume are those that connect different Copenhagen districts with the inner city, roads that go through the city, or roads that connect Copenhagen with surrounding areas.

When was the data collected?

In the ideal scenario, we'd like to work with a uniformly distributed dataset , which means we'd like to have a similar number of counts for each street over time. Visual inspection reveals that some streets do not have continuous data collection. Roskildevej, for example, only has data for four out of the ten years for which data is available. Others, such as Åboulevard and Hareskovvej, have low percentages of year days covered in general. When comparing yearly and monthly patterns between streets, we must keep this in mind because the data for these streets will not be as representative as that for other streets such as Restads Boulevard or Torvegade. Finally, as streets have different collection frequencies every year, we can't compare streets in terms of total sum of counts but in terms of average daily counts.

How reliable is the data?

Not only counting frequency was different for each street, but some rows presented all-zero counts throughout the day or missing counts. Fortunately, the amount of rows with missing counts was relatively low (0.91%), as well as the number of rows with all-zero counts (0.36%).

Patterns can be identified in the way data is missing. In the graph below, we can see that data is missing always in March and October. Analyzing the day and period of the day of these rows we realized that these coincided with the time change!

Patterns can also be found in all-zero counts rows. The presence of these rows seems to occur for some streets in the same period of time, or for the same street for consecutive periods of time. Therefore, as most of the all-zero counts are part of a pattern on a specific period of time, we could treat them as miscounts or outliers that related to counting and consider them as missing data as well.

What happened from 2005 to 2006?

The average of daily passing motor vehicles has been decreasing over the years. The significant decrease from 2005 and 2006 is mainly provoked by the parking strategy adopted by the Copenhagen City Council P-strategy 2005. This strategy consisted in increasing the pay-parking areas, reducing the on-street parking spaces in the inner metropolitan districts, and increasing the underground and on-street parking spaces for residents. Furthermore, parking charges were raised in in the following years, as well as the residence licence. The strategy managed to significanly reduce the number of parked cars in the inner city, as well as the parking time, both for business parkers and private parkers. After the implementation of the strategy, the proportion of work commuters by car had fallen around an 8% in 2009, whereas the proportion of commuters by train had increased around a 9%. These proportions are higher for those who live and work in different places.

How traffic volume varies for each street?

When analyzing temporal patterns by street, we see that almost all of them maintain the same pattern over time. As expected, those streets with the highest traffic volume are main streets that serve as a connection between Copenhagen areas (e.g. Åboulevard and Fredensbro, connecting Nørrebro and the old town), roads that go through the inner city (Kalvebod Brygge), or roads that connect Copenhagen to surrounding areas (Røskildevej, Hareskovvej, Ellevjervej).

But, what is the real variation in average of daily passing vehicles between years and months?

As we can see in the graph above, the traffic volume significantly decreases in the months of July and December with respect to the mean between the months for all the streets. Exactly, these are the months when commuters are on vacation!

With respect to the year variation, we can see that, in general, there is a tendency towards a lower traffic volume, specially important in the case of those streets with higher proportions of motor vehicle traffic such as Åbolevard or Fredensbro, and which are located in the inner city. For some other streets such as Molchesvej or Ørestads Boulevard, the opposite occurred. The latter is the main street of an area that has been gaining in inhabitants for the last 10 years, which may explain the increase in traffic volume.

Look at those peaks! They swap!

During the evening rush hour, traffic out of the city exceeds traffic entering the city. Similarly, during the morning rush hour, traffic entering the city exceeds traffic out of the city. This way, we could say that some of the drivers that arrive in the morning stay in the city until the evening, and those who leave in the morning stay out of the city until the evening. During the weekend, when traffic is significantly lower, rush-hour peaks vanish. Therefore, we could assume that commuters play a key role in the variation of traffic during the week.

Predicting Bike Traffic Volume

A Data-Driven Approach to Design Campaigns and Urban Strategies

To better understand which variables have the most impact on bike traffic volume, we trained two machine learning models based on these attributes:

Month of the year

Day of the week

Time of the day

Street name

Direction of the lane

Could we reliably predict the expected amount of bycicles based on the five given attributes? Could we identify the street, day of the week, time of day, and month when more people will notice our cycling campaign if we had to select when to increase the resources allocated to it? Could we figure out what day of the week and what hour of the day a construction would have the least impact on a street's bike drivers?

Classification Using a Decision Tree Classifier

We divide the traffic volume into the following classes:

Empty Lane: 0-60 byciclists per hour (i.e 1-3 bycicles per minute).

Sparse Traffic: 60-180 byciclists per hour (i.e 1-3 bycicles per minute)

Moderate Traffic: 180-600 byciclists per hour (i.e 3-10 bycicles per minute)

Busy traffic: 600-1200 byciclists per hour (i.e 10-20 bycicles per minute)

Rush hour: more than 900 bycicles per hour (i.e more than 20 bycicles per minute)

The model is now trained to classify samples correctly. The depicted confusion matrix gives insight into the performance of our model: Our model classifies correctly 74% of the samples in the training data set. Further,it is visible that our model is better at predicting samples in the classes with a large number of samples. The model tend to "underestimate" the samples that belonged to classes with high amount of counts.

Regression Using a Random Forest Regressor

This time the model is trained to estimate the bicycle traffic counts as good as possible without classifying them. The resulting model had a mean absolute error of 185, which means that on average the model predicted 185 bikes more (or less) than the actual number of expected bicycles.

The feature importance of the trained model gives us intresting insights into the importance of the attributes for predicting the traffic volume: The most important factor is the time period, followed by the streetname of highly frequented streets and the direction in which cyclists are going. But also the day of the week and the month is significant for the regression model.
